Grantmaking Around the World

Doubling the geographic footprint and issuing 90-120 grants annually through the Cummins Foundation

  • Problem

    Established in 1954, the Cummins Foundation has been supporting community growth around the world for over 60 years. The Cummins Foundation primary funding model differs than most corporate foundations because the overwhelming majority of Community Development Grants are written by Cummins employees for the communities where Cummins employees live and work.

    When I began managing the grant portfolio, there were a few barriers to success. The foundation's applications were outdated, the application process was lengthy, and there was no formal audit or evaluation process after funding was granted. Additionally, the Cummins Foundation was designed to support global grantmaking. Cummins has operations in 160+ countries but the Cummins Foundation had only engaged in 16 of those countries (with most of the portfolio focused on the United States). 

  • Solution

    A project team with representation from each region joined me for a six-sigma project to improve the process from beginning to end. After extensive stakeholder interviewing, process mapping, and a variety of evaluation tools (cause-and-effect matrix, failure modes and effects analysis, a voice of customer KJ, and functional analysis), I designed a new process for submitting grants. We used a Pugh chart to further identify improvements that led to a redesigned Community Development Grant application, a new process for tracking progress, and quality assurance checkpoints built into the final process. After the improvements, I launched two additional projects to create more agility in the foundation to meet the needs of our nonprofit partners around the world. First, a project team worked to move the entire grant process online. Second, I partnered with our internal auditing function to create a process for auditing investments and tracking progress. IN addition to the process improvements, I had the pleasure of managing the Cummins relationship with GlobalGiving, our partners in vetting grantees outside of the United States. Working collaboratively, we laid the groundwork to deepen Cummins impact and partnership with the organization.

  • Impact

    With these changes, we were able to double the number of countries in the Cummins portfolio (from an average of 16 countries to an average of over 30 countries). The process supported the evaluation and approval of between 90-120 grants annually and the approval time for these grant requests from the Foundation was cut in half. Additionally, the grant audit process maintains high standards of quality, strategic focus, and community impact. Most importantly, the CUmmins Foundation was able to increase the number of investments with key partners - which meant more clean water in India, more girls educated in Africa, more food for the hungry in Europe, and more low-income students exposed to STEM programming in the United States.

Executive Diversity and Inclusion Strategy

Coaching the creation and monitoring the implementation of diversity and inclusion plans made by 17 Cummins officers

  • Problem

    Cummins has a long legacy of putting diversity and inclusion at the center of its business. Cummins got involved in the United States Civil Rights movement in the 60s', withdrew from South Africa due to apartheid in 1980, started training its officers on diversity in 1999, and founded its first Employee Resource Group in 2001. IN 2017, Cummins updated its corporate values to include both diversity and inclusion. Knowing that effective engagement in D&I requires leadership engagement, Cummins CEO Tom Linebarger asked each of his officers to create a five-year diversity and inclusion strategy for their business, function, or area of the world.

  • Solution

    Because diversity and inclusion touches each part of the employee experience (when they are hired, how they are developed, what opportunities are available to advance their career, and their decision to stay with a company), it's hard to scope plans effectively. My team worked with each individual leader to scope their plan, deliver suggested improvements, analyze their demographic data to highlight trends, and benchmarked externally to create a final result that was actionable. When designing an effective diversity and inclusion strategy, there needs to be an emphasis on indivual accountability, communications, data and metric analysis, and specific actions. I designed a template for the leaders to use to meet those goals. After the initial creation of plans, I designed a process for quarterly reviews of data and implementation progress. We also designed a report for the CEO to review progress on a quarterly basis.

  • Impact

    Each of the 17 Cummins Officers have created five-year strategic Diversity and Inclusion plans. Additionally, each officer receives a diversity and inclusion dashboard each quarter then summarizes their D&I activities to the Cummins CEO. The quarterly evaluation process allows our team to identify trends, share best practices, provide individual coaching, or brainstorm ways to scale projects that have worked on a local level. Our team now has the tools to coach leaders at other levels of the company on how to create effective diversity and inclusion strategies. This process will sustain D&I efforts and accountability for years to come. The combined efforts have made substantial improvements in making Cummins a more inclusive place to work.

Reversing the Dropout Trend

Reducing the number of high school drop-outs in Bartholomew County by 59%

  • Problem

    iGrad is a program that pairs students at-risk of dropping out of high school with a graduation coach, a mentor, and a tutor to ensure they receive academic, social, or other support needed to graduate and prepare for what’s next. In 2014, the program had been in operation as a pilot for three years. While the program had initial success with preventing drop-out for participants, the organization faced a lack of sustainable funding, a poor volunteer experience, a limited evaluation of its impact or successes, and an uncertain future. With a waiting list of over 200 students in-need of its services, iGrad needed clarity on how to move forward.

  • Solution

    To keep all parties aligned and to best serve the iGrad students, we needed to create a comprehensive, organizational strategic plan detailing how the iGrad would grow. Leading a collaborative, cross-organizational team we defined the project scope and redefined the mission of the iGrad organization. Collecting voices from key community stakeholders, I designed a new organizational structure and model for financial sustainability. One key component of the strategic planning process was the creation of a comprehensive data tracking dashboard (to be used for program effectiveness measurement, fund development proposals, and deepening support provided to students). Before the strategic planning process in 2014, the iGrad program leaders were only monitoring limited data, which prevented program growth. Moving forward, iGrad now can demonstrate success from post-pilot onward.

  • Impact

    In the years since the strategic plan was created, iGrad has accomplished the following milestones:
    - Increased capacity to serve 30% more students
    - Reduce the number of student dropouts in Bartholomew County by 59%
    - Nearly 250% more students graduate annually than from when iGrad began (a 137% increase from the strategic planning process)
    - New organizational designed increased students pursuing post-graduate options by 123%
    - iGrad has more than doubled the number of students successfully enrolled in the 21st Century Scholars
    - Expansion of services to three additional counties

Designing for Disability Inclusion

Designing a workforce strategy for disability inclusion

  • Problem

    The Cummins Leadership Team wanted to engage with the global community of people with disabilities in a more strategic, coordinated and impactful way. Due to the high number of places the company could invest in engaging, without setting some boundaries on what Cummins want to accomplish and how, potential to impact would be diluted. The leadership team wanted a plan that outlined 1) how Cummins would l change the internal environment and culture to make more proactive accommodations and create an inclusive work environment for people with disabilities. 2) how Cummins would communicate this work and strategy and 3) how Cummins will empower other companies to join in our inclusion journey.

  • Solution

    Working with a global project team, I created a three-year roadmap for disability inclusion for our company. Table1 on the established base of disability inclusion, I engaged global and functional stakeholders to determine where to invest resources. In its final form, the roadmap contains annual milestones from 9 different global functions detailing the objectives, estimated costs, and key performance indicators to measure for success. During the development, I led collaboration meetings with external experts, outlined how to continue existing local work, and used Human Centered Design principals to identify focused themes critical for successful implementation.

  • Impact

    Cummins appointed a full-time member of the Global Diversity and Right Environment team to lead from roadmap to implementation. The company has begun to establish external partnerships, intensify recruitment efforts, and further using the network of Disability Inclusion ERGs to make Cummins a more inclusive place to work for all.

Building a Pipeline of Board Volunteers

Building a sustainable pipeline of nonprofit Board Volunteers

  • Problem

    In Columbus, Indiana, a small handful of the same community members were asked to serve in Board positions for nonprofit organizations in town. Additionally, when surveyed, existing Boards rated their value and productivity as a 7 out of 10. When surveyed, nonprofit Executive Directors only rated their Board's effectiveness as 4 out of 10. This reveals two main problems:
    1) A lack of a pipeline with qualified and trained professionals ready to serve in Board leadership positions and
    2) a knowledge gap of effective Board governance among existing nonprofit Boards

  • Solution

    I led a team of volunteers to develop and implement Board Bootcamp. Board Bootcamp is a 12 hour training designed to introduce critical knowledge needed to serve as an effective member of a nonprofit Board of Directors. Over three months, we train participants on legal responsibilities of a Board, financial responsibilities, strategic planning, fundraising, volunteer & stakeholder management, and Board Culture. We pair the training with organizational "Speed Dating" to introduce participants to the Executive Directors of the nonprofit organizations they are interested in serving. I designed the training content, solicited feedback from community stakeholders, and partnered with United Way of Bartholomew County to ensure the sustainability of the program moving forward.

  • Impact

    Since its inception, we've trained over 75 volunteers in Bartholomew County and delivered over 150 hours of individualized Board training and effectiveness consulting to organizations. There is now a developed pipeline of talent for Executive Directors to recruit from when seeking talent, which leads to more diverse and effective boards. The cohort design of the program creates a network for participants to rely on as they begin to serve organizations.

Supporting 100+ Employee Resource Groups

Empowering a network of 150+ Employee Resource Groups to make a more inclusive work environment

  • Problem

    Employee Resource Groups (ERGs) are voluntary, employee-led groups that serve as a resource for members and organizations by fostering a diverse, inclusive workplace aligned with the Cummins mission, vision, and values. Cummins has over 150 chapters of ERGs operating around the world. However, each chapter operated independently of each other with very little information sharing, standard operating processes, or collective impact.

  • Solution

    I took a global audit of the network of ERG leaders to identify the top priority areas for improvement and additional training. Next, I engaged company leadership to determine what capacity needed to be incorporated into the network of ERGs to best serve company goals. Finally, a prototyped version of the guidelines were shared and workshopped with a selected group of ERG leaders. The final design incorporates needs of all partners in diversity and inclusion. To further support the guidelines, my team designed a structure to create regional and global support councils that will expand the reach and impact of ERGs.

  • Impact

    The Operational Guidelines were released. 350+ employees in leadership positions attended trainings over six months to improve how their group operates. In 2018, Cummins ERGs offered 526 events that were attended by 8,707 employees around the world. 5,000+ employees are on the mailing lists of ERGs and the network of groups are consistently asked by company leaders to solve critical problems facing the future of Cummins. Further planned improvements will build on this foundational groundwork.

Making Cities Healthier

Managing public-private partnerships to make 85+ cities healthier for their citizens

  • Problem

    Playful City USA is a recognition program honoring cities and towns that ensure that kids in their communities, particularly kids from low-income families, get the balanced and active play they need to thrive. Supported in partnership with the Humana Foundation, KaBOOM! needed to increase capacity to recognize excellent cities, plan a leadership conference, publicize and share best practices for livable communities, and increase cross-sector collaboration for public play spaces.

  • Solution

    Joining the KaBOOM! staff after completing an internship allowed me to work with Mass Action team to increase the impact of the Playful City USA program. Through the establishment of a comprehensive social media outreach plan and authoring success strategies to build sustainable partnerships. Additionally, our team activated 213 cities to share success strategies, participate in trainings, establish joint-use space proposals, and apply for continual recognition with KaBOOM! Finally, we planned the first annual Playful City USA Leaders Summit.

  • Impact

    By connecting 89 active cities ranging in size from 500 citizens to 8 million citizens, the Playful City USA program helped the private, public, and nonprofit sector create healthier communities. By 2017, 258 communities met the criteria for recognition in the program. In 2018, the Playful Cities USA program retired but KaBOOM! continues to use its national footprint to engage corporate stakeholders, assist elected officials, and manage volunteers across the United States.
